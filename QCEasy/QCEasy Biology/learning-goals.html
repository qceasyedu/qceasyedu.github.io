Learning Goal Breakdown

Topic 1: Describing Biodiversity

Subject Matter

  • Biodiversity:
    • Recognise that biodiversity includes the diversity of species and ecosystems.
    • Determine diversity of species using measures such as species richness, evenness, percentage cover, percentage frequency, and Simpson’s diversity index.
    • Use species diversity indices, species interactions (predation, competition, symbiosis, disease), and abiotic factors (climate, substrate, size/depth of area) to compare ecosystems across spatial and temporal scales.
    • Explain how environmental factors limit the distribution and abundance of species in an ecosystem.
  • Mandatory Practical:
    • Determine species diversity of a group of organisms based on a given index.
  • Notional Time: 9 hours
  • Additional Guidance:
    • Use local context throughout the unit to develop the content objectives.
    • Diversity indices and measurements should be supported through fieldwork and based on classification.
  • Suggested Practical:
    • Measure abiotic factors in the classroom using field samples (e.g., pH, nitrogen nutrients, salinity, carbonates, turbidity).
    • Measure abiotic factors in the field (e.g., dissolved oxygen, light, temperature, wind speed, infiltration rate).

Classification Processes

  • Recognise that biological classification can be hierarchical and based on different levels of similarity of physical features, methods of reproduction, and molecular sequences.
  • Describe the classification systems for similarity of physical features (the Linnaean system), methods of reproduction (asexual, sexual — K and r selection), molecular sequences (molecular phylogeny — also called cladistics).
  • Define the term clade.
  • Recall that common assumptions of cladistics include common ancestry, bifurcation, and physical change.
  • Notional Time: 12 hours
  • Additional Guidance:
    • Students should understand that the concept of classification is directly related to the purpose for which the data will be used.
    • Students should recognise that the Linnaean system does not rely solely on physical features for classification.
    • Classification should be supported by the analysis of field data.
    • Identification of applications of molecular phylogeny (DNA barcoding and genetic testing) should be linked to understanding of subject matter in Unit 4.

Science as a Human Endeavour (SHE)

  • Technology as a tool to measure, analyse and monitor biodiversity: Advances in remote sensing radar imagery and satellite tracking in real time have enabled scientists to measure and monitor populations, and play a significant role in surveying and monitoring large or inaccessible ecosystems.
  • International biodiversity protection: International agreements about biodiversity protection, such as the World Heritage Convention, are based on the premise that local, regional, and international biodiversity represent a global resource, vital for human survival, that should be maintained for future generations.
  • Biodiversity targets: Setting agreed biodiversity targets is required to achieve positive international action towards biodiversity conservation by reducing the rate of biodiversity loss at global, regional, and national levels.

Topic 2: Ecosystem Dynamics

Functioning Ecosystems

  • Sequence and explain the transfer and transformation of solar energy into biomass as it flows through biotic components of an ecosystem.
  • Analyse and calculate energy transfer (food chains, webs, and pyramids) and transformations within ecosystems.
  • Construct and analyse simple energy-flow diagrams illustrating the movement of energy through ecosystems.
  • Describe the transfer and transformation of matter as it cycles through ecosystems (water, carbon, and nitrogen).
  • Define ecological niche in terms of habitat, feeding relationships, and interactions with other species.
  • Analyse data to identify species (including microorganisms) or populations occupying an ecological niche.
  • Define keystone species and understand the critical role they play in maintaining the structure of a community.
  • Notional Time: 12 hours
  • Suggested Practical:
    • Study the abundance of each trophic level in a simple food chain.
    • Measure the wet biomass of producer samples.

Population Ecology

  • Define the term carrying capacity.
  • Explain why the carrying capacity of a population is determined by limiting factors (biotic and abiotic).
  • Calculate population growth rate and change (using birth, death, immigration, and emigration data).
  • Use the Lincoln Index to estimate population size from secondary or primary data.
  • Analyse population growth data to determine the mode of population growth (exponential growth J-curve, logistic growth S-curve).
  • Discuss the effect of changes within population-limiting factors on the carrying capacity of the ecosystem.
  • Notional Time: 4 hours

Changing Ecosystems

  • Explain the concept of ecological succession (refer to pioneer and climax communities and seres).
  • Differentiate between the two main modes of succession: primary and secondary.
  • Identify the features of pioneer species (ability to fixate nitrogen, tolerance to extreme conditions, rapid germination of seeds, ability to photosynthesise) that make them effective colonisers.
  • Analyse ecological data to predict temporal and spatial successional changes.
  • Predict the impact of human activity on the reduction of biodiversity and on the magnitude, duration, and speed of ecosystem change.
  • Notional Time: 8 hours
  • Suggested Practical:
    • Select and appraise an ecological surveying technique to analyse species diversity between two spatially variant ecosystems of the same classification (e.g. a disturbed and undisturbed dry sclerophyll forest).

Science as a Human Endeavour (SHE)

  • Aboriginal knowledge and Torres Strait Islander knowledge of ecosystem interactions and change: Aboriginal communities and Torres Strait Islander communities have knowledge of environmental change and interactions between abiotic and biotic elements of ecosystems in their local contexts.
  • Marine reserves: Scientific knowledge based on local data collection and analysis, computer simulation of future scenarios, and analysis of analogous scenarios is required to analyse the unique factors that affect marine ecosystems.
  • Keystone species and conservation: Keystone species can be more effective as a conservation strategy to maintain complex ecosystem dynamics compared with other strategies such as conservation of flagship species and umbrella species.

  • Topic 1: DNA, Genes, and the Continuity of Life

    • DNA Structure and Replication
      • Understand that DNA is a double-stranded molecule bound to proteins in chromosomes in the nucleus, and as unbound circular DNA in prokaryotes, mitochondria, and chloroplasts in eukaryotes.
      • Recall the structure of DNA, including nucleotide composition, complementary base pairing, and weak hydrogen bonds between DNA strands.
      • Explain the role of helicase and DNA polymerase in the replication process (direction of replication included).
      • Suggested practical: Extract DNA from strawberries, kiwifruit, or wheat germ.
      • SHE: Understand the contributions of Watson, Crick, and Franklin to the double-helix model.
    • Cellular Replication and Variation
      • Recognise the role of homologous chromosomes in meiosis I and II.
      • Describe the processes of crossing over and recombination and how they contribute to genetic variation.
      • Compare spermatogenesis and oogenesis with respect to haploid and diploid cells.
      • Demonstrate how independent assortment and random fertilisation alter offspring genotype variation.
      • SHE: Discuss genetic screening technologies like preimplantation genetic diagnosis and CRISPR in reproductive technologies.
    • Gene Expression
      • Define genome and gene.
      • Understand that genes include coding (exons) and noncoding DNA (tRNA, centromeres, telomeres, introns).
      • Explain the process of protein synthesis (transcription and translation). Include details about mRNA, tRNA, codons, and anticodons.
      • Recognise that gene expression synthesises functional proteins or RNA and can be regulated by various factors.
      • Example of transcription factor gene: HOX family and SRY gene (sex determination).
      • Notional time: 6 hours.
    • Mutations
      • Identify mutations (point, frameshift, non-disjunction, and those caused by mutagens like UV radiation).
      • Explain non-disjunction and how it leads to aneuploidy (e.g., trisomy 21).
      • Use human karyotypes to identify ploidy changes and predict genetic disorders.
    • Inheritance
      • Predict genotype and phenotype frequencies using models like Punnett squares, considering inheritance patterns (autosomal dominant, sex-linked, multiple alleles).
      • Define polygenic inheritance and predict genotype/phenotype frequencies for three alleles (e.g., ABO blood types, wheat grain colour).
    • Biotechnology
      • Describe the process of making recombinant DNA (isolation, cutting, insertion, joining, and amplification).
      • Recognise applications of DNA sequencing for genome mapping and DNA profiling for identification.
      • Explain PCR and gel electrophoresis.
      • Appraise data from biotechnology techniques to assess their success (e.g., DNA banding, PCR effectiveness).
      • SHE: Analyse the implications of DNA profiling for individuals.
    • Science as a Human Endeavour (SHE)
      • Bioinformatics can be used to analyse biological data (e.g., the Human Genome Project).
      • A $1000 genome: Low-cost sequencing helps identify gene variants linked to diseases.
      • Genetically modified organisms: The potential for GMOs in agriculture and pharmaceuticals.

  • Topic 2: Continuity of Life on Earth

    • Evolution
      • Define evolution, microevolution, and macroevolution.
      • Determine episodes of evolutionary radiation and mass extinctions from an evolutionary timescale (approx. 3.5 billion years).
      • Interpret data (e.g., DNA similarity) to reveal phylogenetic relationships and understand comparative genomics as evidence for evolution.
      • Notional time: 3 hours.
    • Natural Selection and Microevolution
      • Recognise that natural selection occurs when environmental pressures confer a selective advantage on a specific phenotype, enhancing its survival (viability) and reproduction (fecundity).
      • Identify that allele frequency selection in a gene pool can be positive or negative.
      • Interpret data and describe three types of phenotypic selection: stabilising, directional, and disruptive.
      • Explain microevolutionary change through mutation, gene flow, and genetic drift.
      • Mandatory practical: Analyse genotypic changes for a selective pressure in a gene pool (via laboratory work or computer simulation).
      • Notional time: 6 hours.
      • Examples of natural selection: beak size in Galapagos finches, antibiotic resistance, insecticide resistance.
      • Mutation is the ultimate source of genetic variation, introducing new alleles to a population.
    • Speciation and Macroevolution
      • Recall that speciation and macroevolutionary changes result from the accumulation of microevolutionary changes over time.
      • Identify four patterns of diversification between species: divergent, convergent, parallel, and coevolution.
      • Describe modes of speciation: allopatric, sympatric, and parapatric.
      • Understand how isolation mechanisms (geographic, reproductive, spatial, and temporal) influence gene flow.
      • Explain how populations with reduced genetic diversity (e.g., those affected by population bottlenecks) face an increased risk of extinction.
      • Interpret gene flow and allele frequency data from different populations to determine speciation.
      • Notional time: 6 hours.
      • Habitat fragmentation: natural and human causes.
      • Reduced genetic diversity and its connection to population bottlenecks (Unit 3 context).
      • Students should determine modes of speciation from data interpretation.
    • Science as a Human Endeavour (SHE)
      • Evidence for evolution: Technological developments in comparative genomics, comparative biochemistry, and bioinformatics provide further evidence for evolutionary relationships.
      • Human evolution: Ongoing evolution in humans, particularly in Western societies, after cultural events like the Industrial Revolution and the introduction of agriculture, modern medicine, and mass transportation.
      • Unsustainable population size and reserve areas: Calculating minimum reserve sizes for conservation species, considering the viability of a single large reserve versus multiple smaller reserves connected by 'green corridors'.
